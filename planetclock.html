Here is a visualization of a solar system model from the JPL page Approximate Positions of the Planets.

The black ring represents a ribbon of sky around the ecliptic plane, that is, the great circle through the fixed stars the Sun traverses each year. The coordinates are J2000.0 ecliptic coordinates, with longitude increasing around the ring, and latitude increasing outward. (Ecliptic longitude is sign of the Zodiac.) The ring is really a thin ribbon standing on edge out of the plane of this page - it has been flattened into the page to be able to draw it in 2D.

The gold circle and line show the position of the Sun relative to the fixed stars. The month ring shows the date at which the Sun reaches each longitude. Drag the Sun point to change its position, and notice that each time you move it from Dec to Jan (or Dec to Jan), the yellow year button increments (or decrements). Click on the year button to jump many years. Notice that the yellow month ring very slowly rotates over the centuries due to precession of the equinoxes. The blue sector represents the part of the sky that is too near the Sun - when the sky is not fully dark after sunset in the evening or before sunrise in the morning - to be able to see any stars (at or below mid-latitudes).

The colored dots represent the five planets visible by naked eye. As you move the Sun around, the planets generally move in the same direction, but periodically reverse direction - called retrograde motion. Mercury and Venus are always near the Sun, slowly overtaking the Sun as they move from morning to evening side, then rapidly retrograde from evening to morning side. Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn move around slower than the Sun, going retrograde as they approach opposition (180 degrees from the Sun, the dim gold line). Unlike the Sun, the planets drift in ecliptic latitude. Click on a planet dot in the central legend to toggle a line between the planet and earth.
